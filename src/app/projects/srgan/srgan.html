Project Description

In this project, my partner Daniel Chen and I built a machine learning model that takes low resolution images and enhances them, generating higher resolution images that contain more visual detail than the original input. We based our approach on the SRGAN (Super-Resolution Generative Adversarial Network) paper, reimplementing the architecture and training process from scratch in Keras.

How GANs Work

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are constructed by taking two Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and pitting them against each other in a never ending game of trying to outsmart the other. One CNN (the generator) is attempting to generate high resolution images from the given low resolution image, and the other CNN (the discriminator) is attempting to detect whether a given image is real, or generated by the Generator. Through their competition, the Generator will start generating more convincing images, and the Discriminator will get better at noticing the flaws that make the image appear fake. Ultimately if the Generator wins the competition, the Discriminator will be unable to determine which images are fake, and which are real.

Architecture and Training

Our generator is a deep residual network: it first compresses the input image into a condensed feature representation through 16 residual blocks, then runs it through two upscaling blocks (using pixel-shuffling) to reconstruct a 4x higher resolution image. The discriminator is a standard deep CNN classifier that takes an image and outputs a single real-vs-fake probability.
One of the more interesting design choices in the SRGAN approach is the loss function. Rather than just comparing pixel values directly between the generated and real high-res images (which tends to produce blurry, averaged-out results), we passed both images through a pretrained VGG19 network and compared their internal feature representations instead. This "perceptual loss" pushes the generator toward textures and details that look right to the human eye, rathan than those that minimize pixel-wise error.
We also split training into two phases: first pretraining the generator by itself using simple pixel-loss to get it into a reasonable starting state, then training it adversarially against the discriminator using the full perceptual loss. The MSE-only phase alone produces images that look sharper but very noisy; layering on the adversarial training afterward smooths everything out into something far more natural looking.

Pitfalls

GANs have a reputation for being notoriously difficult to train, and we found out why firsthand. Our early attempts ran into just about every classic GAN failure mode:
  • Mode Collapse: When the generator learns the average input that produces the least error and only generates that input.
  • Diverging Pixel Values: Output images converging on pure white / black images or saturated colors instead of a real image (one iteration produced a solid magenta square regardless of input)
  • Bad Loss Configuration: Early versions before we'd correctly implemented the VGG perceptual loss which caused the GAN to not converge at all.
Getting past these required a handful of standard GAN stabilization tricks: adding noise to the discriminator's input, occasionally flipping training labels, and training the discriminator less frequently than the generator so it didn't overpower it early on. After enough tuning, we started getting outputs that much more closely resembled the true output images, while also being noticeably clearer than the input.

Limitations

We trained on a relatively small dataset (a few thousand images) for about five weeks on personal desktop hardware. The original SRGAN paper trained on hundreds of thousands of images over millions of iterations using dedicated GPU hardware, so our results, while a clear improvement over the low-res inputs, don't reach that same level of fidelity. With more data, more compute, and more time, the same approach should scale to much better results.
